On being the right size

Digitisation doesn't make everything equal, it just makes everything the same size.

Typically, we are offered more pixels per millimetre for a very tiny object than for a very large one. The camera gets closer to small things, because it can. Across a diverse collection, there probably isn't a consistent dots per real-world inch policy for stamps, coins, printed books, paintings and tapestries.

There's only so much zooming you can do. Capturing The Night Watch at 50 pixels per millimetre is a major undertaking. Capturing a postage stamp at this resolution is regular macro photography or scanning.

Our web pages have spaces for images, we tend to fill them we the images we are given. Both of these Holbeins have more detail available than the arbitrary 800 pixel width shown here. We could try to use the pixel dimensions of the images to give some sense of relative scale.

This scale is obviously hopelessy wrong. At least The Ambassadors comes out bigger than Anne of Cleves in this case. There's no reason for this to be generally true, especially here where the two images are coming from different places.

Even if we know how big things are from written descriptions, they are presented using the same number of pixels on a web page. And if there is deep zoom, we're still using the same viewport from one item to the next. Maybe you can spread your fingers or spin your mousewheel more for some images than for others, get deeper into them; only context, experience and familiarity give you a clue how big something really might be. The exciting tininess of a miniature, the imposing macho spread of _The Ambassadors_ - they both become pixels in an image tag.

This is hardly a new problem. Loss of absolute scale is a tradeoff that lets more people look at art through printed reproductions too.

Can IIIF help? Not really. We're still sitting in front of our monitors and looking at our phones. But there are two ways in which IIIF can do something with scale, that allows two objects to be presented with an idea of their actual sizes.

The first is the Physical Dimensions service. This is an extra piece of metadata a publisher can provide, that tells a software client what the physical scale is - how many pixels in the image make up a millimeter in the real world. Software clients typically use this information to show a dynamic ruler:

The other approach, which ends up at the same place as the above example, is to compose a IIIF Canvas with two images on it at the correct relative sizes.

Rather than giving two independent IIIF resources to the client, we could _place_ the items on a Canvas.
